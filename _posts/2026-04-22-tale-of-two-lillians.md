title: "Tale of two Lillians"
date: 2026-04-22
---
![Tale of two Lillians](/images/2026-04-02-tale-of-two-lillians.jpg)
My Aunty Lily was my Nanna's oldest sister. So technically she was my Great Aunt.

When I think of her the phrase  'lady like' springs to mind.In fact she had a bit of a Queen Mother vibe about her.

Aunty Lily loved things to be done correctly - the table was always set for meals with linen napkins and fine china crockery.

She took a great interest in life and loved people. Gardening was a passion of Lily's and I remember her plants looking healthy and lush.

What made all of these things so extraordinary was my Aunt's disability - she was legally blind. Loss of vision started when Lily was very young - in her 20's. After the birth of her daughter, Aunty Lily's eye sight deteriorated further and continued to decline.

Not once did I ever hear any talk of self pity. Lily's strength of character, faith and determination to keep pursuing her passions motivated and sustained her.

When I left home at seventeen to study in a city several hours away where Lily lived, my relationship with my Aunt became more of a granddaughter/grandma dynamic.This was especially so after my lovely Nanna left us - Lily had no grandkids of her own.

She and my Uncle lived not far from the Uni I attended and I visited often. Hanging out at their place gave me a sense of connection and comfort at a time of huge change in my life.

After I ditched my Behavioural Science degree and started General Nursing, I would often stay with Aunty Lily and Uncle Colin on weekends.On Sunday mornings we always attended the Baptist Church. I found the simplicity and traditions a welcome break from the emotional intensity of the Pentecostal church I had joined.

Lily had also been a nurse before she married and took a great interest in my nursing studies and experiences.

Despite her lack of sight, Lily could navigate her kitchen and general housework with expertise.

Ironing was a serious business and literally everything from tea towels to pillow cases were thoroughly pressed!

I vividly remember watching my Aunt iron, fascinated by the pleats she inadvertently pressed into the fabric. It gave me the 'tingles' which we now refer to as ASMR. Perhaps she could've had her own ASMR Youtube channel if she was still with us!

I loved hearing stories of when she was a girl - especially stories that included things about my Nanna.

During this time my mother and sister moved to the city I now called home and we all lived together.

Whenever Aunty Lily would phone we knew it would be a very long conversation - Lily loved to talk!

My mum devised a clever system  to extricate herself from these marathon calls. Either my sister or I would knock on a wall and  in response our dog would bark loudly. Mum would then say she had to go because there was someone at the door. It worked every time! Did Aunty Lily ever wonder why visitors always turned up when she phoned?

Birthdays were important to Aunty Lily. She always sent birthday cards to family far and wide. She even continued to send cards to my children. Uncle Colin or my cousin would write in the cards on behalf of Lily.

As I mentioned before Lily was very much a lady. But in a strange twist on this almost regal aspect of my Great Aunt, she would serve up food that Uncle Colin found at the local rubbish dump!

He loved to bring home damaged items and repair them. But when his scavenging turned to food it was concerning.

I remember seeing chocolate cake still in its commercial wrapping with a big red X written on it. Did Uncle Colin omit to tell his wife about that big red X?

There were cans of food similarly marked as well. Perhaps Lily's sense of smell was so acute she could detect whether the expired bounty was still safe to consume.

They ate the food though and didn't ever get sick as far as I know!

Uncle Colin was a crazy driver. Being a passenger in his car was quite a hair raising experience. I had the privilege of traveling several hours in their car once, seated in the back, tossed around as Colin turned corners at breakneck speed.

Aunty Lily, settled calmly in the front seat, seemed oblivious to the pace and erratic driving. We arrived at our destination in one piece, although I recall feeling nauseated with travel sickness for awhile.

Speaking of nausea, there was one breakfast that Aunty Lily served frequently that made me queasy. Most of her cooking was delicious - her Christmas plum pudding springs to mind. This is remarkable considering her disability.

But back to breakfast. I can still see the pale, gelatinous smooth strips covered in white parsley sauce, thankfully served with toast. This deliciosy is known as tripe, the edible lining from the stomach of cows, pigs and sheep.

When I tried to eat this substance an overwhelming feeling of wanting to gag would hit me.

This was one time I was grateful Aunty Lily couldn't see my face. I'd manage to eat a few pieces of the strange chewy stuff by mixing it with huge bites of toast.

Even though I could never finish this meal, claiming I was full, Aunty Lily would always ask if I wanted more tripe. I never had the heart to tell her how much I didn't like it because I knew how much effort had gone into making this breakfast for me - and I loved her. 

The other Lillian in my life is my amazing youngest daughter. When our baby girl decided to arrive several weeks before her due date on Aunty Lily's birthday, it seemed that naming her Lillian was meant to be.

Our Lilly is strong, resilient and kind just like her namesake. She has dealt with many challenges in the her life too.

It would be wonderful to think that somehow Aunty Lily knows that her name lives on in a young woman who possesses many of her attributes.

I'm so grateful to share genes with these inspirational women, two remarkable Lillians.
