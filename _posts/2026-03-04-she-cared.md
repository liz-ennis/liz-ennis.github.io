![She Cared](/images/2026-03-04-she-cared.jpg)

I grew up in a small country town. The Baptist Church featured heavily in my childhood. I actually attended Sunday services while in utero! (I like to call this being marinated in fundamentalism.)

Sunday school, Christian Endeavour, youth group and Sunday services were an integral part of our lives.

Sitting next to my Nanna in church has always been a happy childhood memory. I could always count on Nanna to have butter menthols in her handbag. And, she would consistently provide paper and pencils for me to draw.The services were not exactly engaging for a child - or anyone else for that matter!

I also have memories of my father sitting on my bed warning me about eternity-that I had to  put things right with God or I'd end up in hell. (Not a particularly comforting bedtime story for little girls!) He also lectured us about 'idle words' ie only talk about things of substance!

My dad was emotionally repressed. He found it incredibly difficult to convey love. The only time he really expressed emotion was when he ' lost it 'and hit us with a leather strap that hung on a nail near our kitchen.

But the threat of hell wasn't my only fear. If I couldn't find my parents or sister panic ensued-the rapture had happened and I'd been left behind! My sister had the same fear. I now know that 'rapture fear' was very common amongst those raised in fundamentalism.

It was an odd way to grow up, absorbing all the Christian ideology but (even as a child) sensing there was something disingenuous going on.

My mother had multiple relationships with other men while married to my dad. I'd come home from school and there would sometimes be a strange man visiting.(They were often ministers of religion.)

In fact my mother had an affair with the pastor of the church we attended.My Dad was completely oblivious and even invited this man to join us on a day trip on our boat.

According to my mother, she went to this minister for help and somehow ended up in a sexual relationship with him. Such an abuse of power, but I'm really not surprised.

The one person in my life who loved me unconditionally - my Nanna, is probably the reason I am relatively normal and functional (neurospiciness not withstanding!)

She wasn't one to gush, but I knew without a doubt that her love for me was rock solid.

When I was born she caught a ride on the ' milk truck' into town every day to spend the day with mum and me. My mother credits Nanna with my early speech.She carried me around and pointed to objects and taught me their names.

When my sister arrived 3.5 years later,my mother actually said to Nanna:'This one is mine.'

So many of my happy childhood memories are connected to the time I spent with my grandparents at their farm.

Nanna had a big container of buttons and a big wad of plastercine. I had many happy hours of creative play with both. She also taught me how to crochet, knit and sew.

I remember sleeping in her room at night. Sometimes she would read stories, but my favourite tales were those she made up - eenie weenie Winnie the doll that came alive.

Nanna was far from perfect but she was a consistent anchor of love in my life.

Sadly, my younger sister didn't have the same connection with our grandmother.But she had a lovely relationship with our grandfather.

My mother was so young when I arrived - just 2 months after her 21st birthday. According to my mum, she wanted to do her nurses training. But my grandparents concerned about her rebellious nature, didn't want her living so far away. They were convinced she would ' go to the devil'. They encouraged her to marry my dad because he was a 'good man who would look after her'.

On her wedding day mum confessed to her father  that she didn't love my father. My grandfather told her that love would come with time.

So considering everything,I think their marriage might have been doomed from the beginning.

Through many conversations, my aunt has given me a better understanding of the younger version of her sister. My mother was always difficult, would burst into rages, upset the whole household and leave.Later she'd return and act as if nothing had happened. My aunt was at her mercy and was made to do lots of awful things - and still loves her sister today.

Knowing all of this (and more) I understand why Nanna wanted to be around for me. Perhaps she felt like I was a second chance?

My sister has had a troubled life with much emotional pain and so many abusive relationships - just like our mother.

I'm incredibly grateful to have had Nanna in my life for 19 years. Her legacy of consistent unconditional love lives within me.

Psychologists say that kids only need one person show up for them consistently in order to be emotionally stable and resilient.

My Nanna provided this foundation for me because she cared.
