There has certainly been some approach/avoidance surrounding this post.It's still challenging for me to write about my father even though he's been dead for 27 years this September.

The photo I've chosen is one of him at work. He worked a lot. Work seemed to be his comfort zone  I think he found more satisfaction fixing tractors than he ever did in time spent with his wife and daughters.

I mentioned in a previous post that he was emotionally repressed.As an adult I've come to understand some of the reasons for his inability to express love.

My paternal grandmother was extremely negative and unkind.When we visited -which thankfully wasn't often as they lived a several hours away- she would tell me I was fat.( Way to go Grandma!)

One of her sayings has become a bit of a family joke. As I kid I once presented her with a drawing I'd created for her. Her response was to give the artwork back to me as she said:'Grandma's not interested'.So we jokingly repeat that phrase to this day.

Grandma gave birth to 8 children, my father was number 7 in birth order. Grandma freely admitted that my dad should never have been born. Apparently she frequently repeated this to my dad throughout his childhood.

Yet,when Grandma's 8th child arrived,this son was showered with affection and could do no wrong.

How to damage your child in 2 easy steps!

Birthdays were never celebrated in my father's childhood. Perhaps this is why he never remembered my birthday or my sister's date of birth.

I'm assuming that corporal punishment was part of dad's upbringing because he used a leather strap on my sister and me. Church teaching about 'sparing the rod and spoiling the child'obviously contributed to this practice as well.

I was 12 when I received my last belting with the leather strap. My sister and I were fighting and as usual dad snapped. He chased me around the grape vine and hit the back of my legs repeatedly with the strap.

I was so angry and humiliated. Even back then writing was a way for me to vent.I wrote pages in my diary about how much I hated Dad and wished he were dead. 

Needless to say, during my childhood,physical affection was scant and declations of love were non-existent.

When I was 16 I discovered Penticostalism and full of enthusiasm I decided I would tell my parents that I loved them.Both of them looked embarrassed and were unable to say the words back to me.

I also have vague memories of my father arriving home from work in his grease stained overalls, but being told not to go near him because he was dirty. After awhile I stopped attempting to greet him with a hug.

For most of his life I believe my father felt misunderstood. It seems likely that he was autistic. Even though I can now see things from a more rational perspective,the little girl in me still feels  sadness and longing for the father/daughter connection I never had.

Even watching movies that portray tender moments between a daughter and her dad will have me reaching for the tissues.

When I separated from my first husband, I got to experience a wide range of negative emotions from my father. I still have all the letters he sent me. ( Why have I kept them? I'm not really sure)

He was incredibly angry at me. It felt as if he were re-living my mother leaving him.

Several years later Dad discovered a red lump on his neck. He sought advice from a GP who dismissed his concerns.

The lump continued to grow so he decided to consult a naturopath who sold him ointment for $100 per jar.

By the time he eventually saw another Doctor, it was too late. The lump-now the size of a small plum-was actually a melanoma.Over time the cancerous cells had inflitrated his internal organs and spine.

Watching my father waste away and turn yellow with jaundice was sad and confronting.But the way my partner and I were treated added another layer of angst. We were made to wait on the closed-in veranda while other family members were inside my Aunt's house.( Where he had chosen to die) No-one offered us a cup of tea or even a glass of water.( Strangely after my father died we were invited for lunch)

It was also insinuated by my dad's sister that I was responsible for his cancer because of all the stress I'd caused him by leaving my marriage.( I'm pretty sure it's the sun that causes melanoma - not me!)

I was pregnant during this time which added to my already overwhelmed emotional state.My step mother did not want my father to know I was having a child with 'the one'- the way they referred to my now husband.

My sister also visited with her fiance-they thought he was great-and tea was on their menu. The relatives thought her new fiance would 'keep my sister in line'.( Yes, really) This guy turned out to be many things, but great wasn't one of them.He cleverly let them think he was religious ( very important). When asked which church he attended he told them he was raised Church of England. But in reality he was an atheist. He and my sister ultimately divorced.( I'm still with 'the one')

So, even on my father's death bed he was still unable to say the words'I love you'.I asked 'Do you love me Dad?'
His response was: 'Of course I do'in an angry tone. Then he went on to say that I had been ' the hope for the family's future ' and now that was gone. This man was literally dying. Did he not think that this would be my last memories of him? Obviously not.

The very last time I saw him he was slipping in and out of consciousness. I held his hand and told him I loved him.His eyes flew open - a vivid bright blue - and he pulled his hand away from mine. Did he see me? Did he know it was me? His emaciated yellow body was full of morphine so I don't know and I never will.

And to add insult to injury, my father completely bi-passed my sister and me in his will. He left money to each of my kids to be distributed when they turned 18. He left everything to my step-mother as a 'life interest'. The will was made from a will-kit, my sister's name was spelt incorrectly and my step- mothers 3 daughters were named before my sister and me - his biological children.Thanks again Dad!

My sadness after his death was more about grieving what could have been, not necessarily missing him.

The love I feel for my parents is convoluted. I've always longed for a less perplexing,smoother, simpler kind of love.

No-one can deny that our childhood shapes us. However,we still have a choice in how we respond to what happens to us - and that can make all the difference.
